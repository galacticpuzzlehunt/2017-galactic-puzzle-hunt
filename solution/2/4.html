Solution: How to Best Write an Essay

Written by Anderson Wang

This puzzle consists of an essay, along with suggested edits from two people: “W” in 1999 and “Y” in 2014. Most of the edits seem to be correcting grammar or usage errors.

There are several places in the essay with slightly awkward wording (“she tried to viciously sue me”, “it’s not enough to yearly write”). These places, as well as the title “How to Best Write an Essay,” all contain a split infinitive, a grammatical construct which some (incorrect!) prescriptivists argue is a usage error. Each paragraph has a split infinitive: in order, they are “to yearly write,” “to always keep,” “to not be,” “to kindly remind,” “to officially receive,” “to viciously sue,” “to irrefutably prove,” “to completely crash,” “to woefully rationalize,” “to honestly be,” “to inwardly live,” “to thoroughly give,” and “to eventually get.”

The first letters of the adverbs in the split infinitives spell out YANKOVICWHITE, which suggests that the Y stands for YANKOVIC and the W stands for WHITE. Through some googling, one can find that the 2014 “Weird Al” Yankovic song “Word Crimes” is about grammar and usage (and also contains a split infinitive). Each of the edits by “Y” corresponds to a lyric in the song, such as “You should know when / it’s ‘less’ or it’s ‘fewer’” for the edit in the first paragraph.

Similarly, WHITE refers to E. B. White, one of the authors of The Elements of Style, a famous style guide. The newest 4th edition was published in 1999, and contains 22 writing guidelines throughout its first two chapters. Each of the edits by “W” corresponds to one of these guidelines.

Converting the Elements of Style numbers into letters and sorting these letters by the position in “Word Crimes” gives the phrase DEBUT KANO SONG. The debut single of rapper Kano is P’S AND Q’S, the answer to this puzzle.

Author's Notes

Most people who testsolved this puzzle skipped the first step and jumped directly to “Word Crimes” and The Elements of Style. For example, googling things like “less fewer nouns prepositions irony coincidence” brings up the Weird Al song, and The Elements of Style is certainly one of the most famous books on grammar and usage. I hope that the split infinitives were of use to at least some teams, though.

I used “W” instead of “S” or “S&W” because if you do get the “YANKOVIC WHITE” message, you should be reasonably confident in looking at Strunk & White, whereas if you don’t, you might need to do more digging to confirm that it’s correct. I also used the 4th edition because previous editions only had 18 guidelines, which is a lot more difficult to make a usable clue phrase with. This is a little annoying because only the 1st edition is public domain, but I assume that everyone has their own copy of the 4th edition and didn’t have to pirate it. ;)

During the hunt, many teams submitted answers like "I'M READY" and "IT'S A WAR." It turns out that there is also an obscure Italian band called Kano that we all missed during testsolving! Apologies to anyone who was affected by this oversight.
